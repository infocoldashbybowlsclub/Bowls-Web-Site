The History of Lawn Bowls

From Royal Bans to the Modern Green

Lawn Bowls is the anglicised variant of a family of ancient rolling sports. Across the globe, this basic concept has taken on numerous distinct regional signatures: Bocce in Italy, Bolla among ancient Saxons, Bolle in Denmark, Boules or Pétanque in France, and Ula Maika in Polynesia.

The standard English variation developed on finely manicured grass lawns—a natural byproduct of England's damp, temperate climate. Conversely, in the hotter climates of France and Italy where pristine grass is harder to sustain, variations evolved to be played primarily on gravel, stone dust, or hard dirt pitches.

Key Distinctions of English Bowls: Unlike continental variations, English bowls are smoothly rolled along the ground rather than thrown through the air. Furthermore, the woods are not perfectly spherical; they are deliberately engineered with an asymmetrical curve, creating a bias that causes the bowl to follow a distinct curved path as it slows down.

1. Ancient Roots and Global Spreads

Some sports historians trace the true ancestry of the game back to ancient Egypt. Spherical stone artefacts have been excavated from tombs dating near 5,000 B.C. While the existence of stone spheres doesn't explicitly prove how they were played, they mark the earliest human fascination with precision rolling games.

Centuries later, a sport played by young medieval Londoners called "casting the stone" was documented during the 12th century by William FitzStephen in his biography of Thomas à Becket. Written in Latin as jactu lapidum, it described players carefully rolling rounded stones toward fixed targets.

The Etymology of the "Jack"

The definitive origin of the word "Jack"—the smaller white target ball—remains a subject of debate among bowling historians. There are three leading theories:

  • The Latin Connection: Deriving from jactus, meaning a cast or a throw.
  • The Traditional Sport: Evolving directly out of the medieval jactu lapidum rock-casting games.
  • The Linguistic Shortcut: In early English, "Jack" was often prefixed to items to indicate a smaller version of something (e.g., jack-rabbit). Thus, a smaller target ball was naturally dubbed a "Jack-Bowl," eventually shortened to simply the Jack. This is backed up by 1697 writings noting a player who "had not strength to throw the Jack-Bowl half over the green."

2. The "Sport of Kings" and Wartime Bans

As the sport grew in popularity throughout medieval England, it drew intense pushback from the Crown. King Edward III (1361) and later King Henry VIII (1541) passed strict legislation banning or heavily restricting lawn bowls among the common working classes.

The concern was strictly military: the Crown worried that critical tradesmen—such as bowyers, fletchers, and arrow-head makers—were spending their leisure hours playing bowls rather than practicing archery, which was vital for national defense.

Henry VIII’s Unlawful Games Act formally banned artisans, laborers, and apprentices from playing except during Christmas holidays, and even then, only under their master's direct supervision. To enforce a class divide, anyone wishing to keep a private bowling green was required to pay an immense fee of £100, and play was limited strictly to private walled gardens or orchards.

3. Drake and the Spanish Armada

The most legendary folklore in bowling history belongs to Vice Admiral Sir Francis Drake. On July 18, 1588, Drake was reportedly in the middle of a match on Plymouth Hoe when lookouts arrived to announce that the warships of the Spanish Armada were visible on the horizon.

His legendary, cool-headed response has been preserved through generations: "We still have time to finish the game and to thrash the Spaniards, too." History records that he finished out his match before setting sail to successfully defeat the invasion. While historians still debate if the quote is romanticized, it remains an iconic symbol of the sport's cultural footprint.

4. The Scottish Rules and Modernization

While England provided the lawns, modern lawn bowls owes its official rulebook to Scotland. During the 19th century, the game experienced a massive resurgence across Scottish communities. In 1864, William Wallace Mitchell, a Glasgow cotton merchant, published the Manual of Bowls Playing, which standardized the dimensions, rules, and scoring systems used globally today.

Simultaneously, industrial engineering transformed play. The invention of the mechanical lawnmower by Edwin Budding in 1830 allowed clubs to easily maintain the perfectly level, razor-thin grass surfaces required for high-precision competitive play.

The W.G. Grace Revolution

At the dawn of the 20th century, legendary English cricketer W.G. Grace turned his massive sporting influence toward bowls. Following his retirement from top-tier cricket, Grace helped convert old tennis courts at Crystal Palace into pristine greens, founding the London County Bowling Club.

In 1903, he served as the founding President of the newly established English Bowling Association (EBA) and captained England's winning squad in the inaugural international series against Scotland, Wales, and Ireland. His cross-sport fame successfully transitioned lawn bowls from an elite, gated pastime into a highly organized, widely accessible national sport.

5. The Structure of the Modern Game

Today, the game is governed globally by World Bowls, standardizing matches across the Commonwealth and beyond. While historically nicknamed "woods," 90% of modern bowls are constructed from high-density composite resins designed to withstand environmental wear while keeping a flawless weight bias.

Matches are organized across multiple formats based on team sizes:

  • Singles: Head-to-head match play where each competitor delivers four bowls alternately.
  • Pairs: Two teams of two players, alternating four bowls per player.
  • Triples: Teams of three players, using three bowls each.
  • Rinks (Fours): The classic four-player team layout, where each player delivers two bowls. Each squad is directed by a "Skip"—the team captain who stands at the target end to analyze the head, chart tactics, and deliver the critical final bowls of the end.

Our Legacy

Cold Ashby Bowls Club

⛰️ 190m Above Sea Level

"The Highest Green in the County"

1921

Cold Ashby Bowls Club is situated on a site at the south west corner of the Cold Ashby playing fields with access from the West Haddon Road opposite Church Lane. The club was originally founded in 1923, the first green being on Home Farm.

1936/37

A New Era: The club moved to a new green on Manor Farm

1939
1996

A Modern Era:The Club moved to the current site in 1996.
It has since been developed into a first class club with a pavilion and separate changing rooms together with an excellently maintained full size bowling green. The bowling season runs from April to September during which time regular friendly matches are played against other clubs in Northamptonshire plus some nearby clubs in Warwickshire and Leicestershire. We also enter mens and ladies teams in the Northamptonshire leagues.
The club is always open to accept new members whether established bowlers or novices for whom coaching can be arranged.

Today

Considered one of the finest greens in the county. Our 620ft elevation adds a unique challenge!
